The day appeared no different from any other random day in a year, at least initially. I woke up from bed, dressed up, had my regular breakfast of cereal and milk, and then I went to school. The day started as usual.

I was in the same spot in the classroom, right next to the window. It was close to break time. The bell knelled ten times. I looked out through the window in fatigue. The morning had been exhausting.

It was probably the first time that day when I got to observe the sky from such a close distance. I opened the window. Beside me, a gigantic cluster of overcasts hovered. It was almost a single gray thing upon the horizon. The far hill faded from my sight. My vision blurred while I was sniffing the air. I found it foreign. No longer did the fresh oxygen please my senses, it was all the smell of rotten flesh of an unknown source.

A certain tension was rising in the classroom. A flying mosquito caught the attention of everyone. At first, it stayed on the uncovered part of Peter’s arm. Shortly after it stung Peter, it got no close to other people to fulfill its unfinished duty. It did not flap its wings aimlessly to wander across the room. A visible attempt of fleeing was captured. On the outside suspended an uncountable swarm of mosquitoes. They made an effort to maintain a distance away from the classroom. I had never seen any similar behaviors from mosquitoes of no intelligence.

The mosquito flew in a straight route to the window, and as it was about to cross its frame, the class started.

Peter shut the window in an immediate temper. I saw the mosquito crushed. Its blood streamed down from the white window frame to the sill, and eventually flowed onto my desk.

It was human blood.

Just then, the huge bunch of mosquitoes outside scattered away.

I took a heavy breath as if to reiterate to myself that nothing was special about the day. While I was holding my breath, a penetrating sharp burst of scream pierced through my eardrum. The sound lasted. Students, and everyone in the classroom building, got provoked. A crowd of different people soon gathered in the hallway outside their classrooms, squinting their eyes to see what was happening on the far end of the campus. It turned out that some severe accident was outbreaking near the school gate. That was the rumor that spread when people still weren’t sure what was truly going on. The motive behind their attention was out of pure curiosity that came naturally with humans.

I too was curious and followed the crowd to the front door of the classroom building to get a closer look. All the way outside the school entrance, I noticed that a tramp-like person was acting with a rather bizarre behavior, pushing his hands across the gaps on the iron-made gate. The security threw some hand gestures at the man, telling him to leave. The man didn’t back up in response, instead, he continued to toss his entire body over the gate. The security could tolerate no more. He held out his hand to the other side of the door.

It was all late.

The tramp with ragged clothing took a heavy bite on the security’s shoulder. His blood is spilling everywhere. The man just wouldn’t let him go. The security was the source of the unending scream. The ground was stained blood red. His expression distorted, and was actively pulling his shoulder out of the man’s mouth. Soon after, he stopped resisting.

The security lay on the floor for so long as what seemed to be an eternity. A group of people was departing in fear, leaving the rest of us watching. Till the time when even I got bored, something happened. The security got back to his feet, with his neck twisted at an inhuman angle. Then he started to progress forward, ignoring the roadblock in front of him. He slipped the moment when he ran into the obstacle, thereby stopping moving. Nobody took an action to show care, not until a courageous classmate of mine stepped up to check his condition. And even by now, I couldn’t recall his name. He ran towards the motionless security, squatting down, looking close to his wound. The filthy blood kept pumping out. The horrifying scene literally stunned my classmate. He retreated a few steps, realizing that the security’s pupils were gone, leaving only the empty eyeball. Something must have gone wrong with him.

But again, it was too late.

More blood splashing.

This time the blood was from my classmate. I had not put the pieces together until I heard his desperate voice.

‘He…He bites me!’ The helpless boy shouted in despair.

My poor classmate rose back from the ground with the quickest speed, not knowing what would later happen to him. The security was coming after him. The security got back on his feet and started walking in a weird posture with his humerus exposed outside. He was chasing after my fleeing classmate.

‘He is coming for me!’ Silence broke down in the classroom building while the boy was running back, the quirky security followed behind.

As the boy sprinted, those who assembled at the front door of the classroom building, watching from a distance through the glass soon dispersed and diminished. None of the pupils at that moment had any intention to rescue him. The door was kept firmly shut. A solitary shadow extended to the outside through the transparency. Mr. Jackson stayed behind the door. His arms folded over his chest. A minute ago, he was helping with evacuating the panicking students. Now that people backed away, he got relieved from multi-tasking and could finally cope with the emergency situation attentively.

Likewise, Mr. Jackson must have also been struggling with finding a resolution. The fastest way was always to block both the boy and the mad security outside. The two were about eight to nine hundred inches away. There were various cartons in the vicinity. The students, including me, could help with moving them around to the door. But Mr. Jackson took little action and made no command. He missed the single best chance to prevent the accident from turning into an appalling hazard. He crouched with a weary face. I could almost tell his mind that he was concerned about my classmate, the enthusiastic class leader whose smile persisted on his face. The flashback soon ended in my mind, and I looked away to the outside again. It was the boy’s face that captured my sight. No longer was that immortal smile visible, the only message that I could read on his face was composed of utter despair and ineffable agony. It was the inferno of endless suffering. I went up another step on the stairway and prayed for him to escape by sheer luck. It was the most pious prayer I had practiced.

Soon and eventually, my classmate got very close to the glazed door. It seemed to me that Mr. Jackson had made up his mind. He proceeded to open the door and waited with infinite patience for the arrival of the boy. I guessed his plan was to segregate him and the security after letting in the former using the just appropriate gap of distance between the two. The boy was quite a way ahead of the security after speed-running for a minute.

At the moment when we all thought Mr. Jackson had drawn a happy ending to the fiasco, a gust of fierce wind stroked the world with some unpleasant intentions, blowing my classmate away from the designated route. It didn’t take too long until he stood up. He rushed again for the door, but the security then got even closer. They are in the distance of an easy reach. The boy stretched his arm to the door with his feet grabbed by the lying security who also seemed to be knocked down by the sudden wind. The cursed man nibbled at the feet, then legs, gradually approaching the upper part of the classmate’s body. Mr. Jackson had realized how big a trouble it was becoming. He couldn’t be that irresponsible to let in a threat despite his desperate will of saving the boy. So he shut the door down with maximized power, rejecting the poor boy. He ordered the students to carry the heavy cartons to the door. We shielded the entrances with them, leaving the outside completely invisible. What was left was that stretched arm clamping in the crevice, still moving, the blood vessel still pumping. The boy was bleeding. Mr. Jackson’s expression remained solemn.

A while after, the threshold became brimmed with a lack of motion. Mr. Jackson tried to reach out for the arm with all meticulousness to examine its condition. At that very moment, another accident broke out. I saw the stiffened hands flapping as if it was in the grip of a convulsion. On the tip of the arm, withered fingernails quickly grew to the shape of a bayonet. They started poking in every direction. Mr. Jackson, though stunned, still managed to accomplish his aim. He grabbed a hand, dragging it close to his face so that he could observe. In a few moments, a staggering wound appeared near his eyebrow regardless of all the effort being made to evade the assault of the fingernails. The arms stopped swaying ultimately.

‘The mobs are subdued!’ said Henry who was watching through the side window.

I regretted having asked Mr. Jackson how he felt. As a response, he announced to all students on the first floor that the injury was ‘not a thing’ and the alarm was all cleared. Back in the first place, it really got us settled down.

People disappeared back to their classrooms in batches. Order restored.

I noticed that Peter was absent during the entire chaos. He was my best friend, and I couldn’t be more familiar with his meddlesome character. It did seem slightly strange. I remembered that he left for the toilet. He wasn’t anywhere in the classroom when I returned. It shouldn’t take so long, I thought.

The bell knelled eleven times. I sat back down in my seat, waiting for the class to start again.

The whole world remained overspread with pale gray dullness. Soon it began to rain cats and dogs. I could withhold the mania at the back of my mind no more because of all matters that had just taken place. Nobody was able to focus on the class content, even the teacher. She was looking around when introducing to us the concept of comedy, not realizing that she constantly misspoke it as ‘tragedy’. Mr. Jackson was stranded on the first floor. He called the police. Miss. Judy came to substitute. It was apparent that she didn’t keep track of our pace and thus did not know where we were at. We’ve discussed about comedy a week ago. It was the correct timing to introduce tragedy according to the syllabus. Her slip of tongue occurred almost unnaturally coincidental.

I extended my neck to the exterior, looking for the usual sensation of relief. It was not there. A drop of rain fell into my mouth, a sour taste. Things were so odd that day.

I decided to take a walk in the hallway and look for Peter in passing. The excuse as usual was that I need to use the bathroom. I found Henry raising his hand before me. He seemed to be in a real emergency. Given that there were only two seats there and Peter was probably occupying one of them, I had to let him go first. I spun my pen while waiting. An ill-omened sense of agitation continued to stream down my heart. I was about to explode.

Henry hurried in quick steps, and I kept on following the null lecture in no mood. The hallway was initially filled with the scurrying of feet but soon returned to complete silence.

Until a boom crushed down the temporary peacefulness from the southwest of the floor. It was the direction of the toilet. Some negative thoughts crossed my mind. The noise might have something to do with Peter! With a foreboding sense in mind, I resolved to go check on him and rushed out.

To be honest, everyone appeared startled due to the barrage of shocks that day, only to different degrees. I was reacting most radically. One after another, it was more than enough surprises for regular campus students.

When I arrived at the toilet, I saw Peter on the floor. Henry stood with blood dripping off his fist. I didn’t witness the process, but a brawl was sure to have turned up in the messy humid space. Peter who lost the combat was cramping. He frothed at the mouth and his eyes were rolling. Henry went on scolding in undertones——it was his wont. He was a naughty one in class but had rarely been involved in actual fighting. The incident must have happened for a reason. So I approached my ear to listen to his murmur, waiting for the truth.

‘This skinny ass bites me. Damn it! You dare do it again, I'mma beat you up.’ Henry swore. One of his arm was then marked by a ring of teeth.

I related the scene back to the accident in the morning. Something in common was the biting behavior. Peter was not a hidden rabid presumably, at least he never bit me in the past three years of our desk-mate career. I recalled the tramp assault that morning. He attacked the security with a bite. Thereafter the watchman grew anomalously aggressive. Was Peter affected by that? He wasn't close to the event site. Was he bit on his way to school? Or…

Was it the mosquitoes？

My thoughts were soon interrupted as Henry in front of me started bounding his head against the wall. I didn’t understand what was going on with him. His whispers turned snappish accompanied by heavy gasps. I asked with concern how he felt. He did not answer but insisted on pushing me backward. Just at that moment, the school radio blared out an urgent announcement from the government.

I couldn’t remember the broadcast any clearer. I should retell it word by word. It wasn’t any sort of lengthy proclamation. It was so brief yet as intense as an explosive.

‘Due to a nuclear leakage, unjustified acts of violence occur among people of all ages everywhere across the world. The typical symptoms of the affected are common aggressive behaviors, which won’t last long. It is not known whether the symptom is contagious.’

I pinched my face to cheer myself up. They said it won’t last long. It should be fine.

Peter, out of the blue, jumped up and bit Henry in the shin as I comforted myself.

They said it won’t last long——

It had been more than one hour.

They claimed that it happens due to a nuclear leakage——

What about those mosquitoes?

It is not known whether the symptom is contagious——

We saw people passing down the symptom!

I cried out in horror instinctively, not expecting the sound to have resonated with one another downstairs. In some other parts of the building, someone must have been experiencing the same. The sound originated from very far below.

My subsequent suffering suggested that by the time that source of the screamer might have been corrupted, and it was not due to either the security or my classmate——they contributed to it obliquely. Mr. Jackson was a vector. He passed down the pathogen by biting on one another, and it kept on spreading exponentially in the same method.

Back to me on the third floor, I had no idea back then how to deal with both Peter and Henry. Obviously, they were the ones who carried the symptom. The difference was that Henry hadn't lost his mind completely. Only God knows what would then happen to him. The alarm went on. The broadcast kept going,

‘An update on instruction: we advise people to stay at home and avoid unnecessary outings; please do exercise moderate self-defense if you are outdoor and are being attacked by people with the above symptoms. They won’t stop attacking autonomously. Please stay where you are and wait for further instructions that shall arrive soon.’

All at once, Henry turned his neck to me, twisting it at an inhuman angle. I noticed that his pupils were gone just like the security. In my mind came a voice. It made a pressing exhortation that was repeated infinite times.

‘Run’

So I ran without looking back, with no hesitation, no destination in my mind up to that point.

Where could I go?

On the way, I thought about the situation and concluded that it might have gone way worse than the government officials had expected. Peter and Henry were two different beings than they were in the past. I didn’t know whether they could ever recover. It also went beyond my capacity to care for their recovery. At the moment, there was only one thing I ought to do, and that was to find a weapon to guard myself. I knew that the equipment room was located on the first floor. It stored a few baseball bats and shot-puts that were likely to be useful. In the activity room of the fencing club which I used to be a member of kept some smooth wooden sticks as tools for practice. They could effectively keep the mobs a distance from me. I also needed a lock to stop people from easily entering where I would shelter myself.

Whilst thinking, I arrived at the second floor. I did not pay attention to Miss. Judy who called for me from a distance. All I did was running, trying to save my own life from this. I didn’t know if they were behind me. I had no time to squander. The top priority was to collect as many tools as possible. I also planned to get some bandages and antibiotics from the nursing room——those were prepared for the worst situation. I put myself into action quickly.

First thing first I intended to check on the equipment room. It was never locked during day times.

I would soon find out that it wasn’t a wise move, from a hindsight perspective.

There I was, at the corner of the staircase. I made sure that they didn’t follow me down to the second floor. I also wished that people on the third floor stayed well. I didn’t tell them anything about Peter and Henry. An ostensible reason for that was because I didn’t want them to panic as it would only result in worse conditions, leaving more people in danger. I had a little self-interest, too. The number of available tools on the campus was so limited. I needed to make sure that I had access to them to defend myself. My own life was my primary task to myself. Sorry, but I couldn’t be that Saint.

I heard ominous growls coming aside, which was why I didn’t dare to act rashly. I got impatient soon and glanced towards the stairs a few minutes later.

Hell no!

The first floor was crowded with infected people. They were ripping each other apart. Mr. Jackson was a part of the group. His phone was on, with its screen glowing. It was stuck on a calling page. The other end of the phone was questioning, but they should never get a response. The door part was tightly sealed. The line of defense stayed integral. It was obvious who the source of the chaos was.

I was so astonished by the scene that I forgot about the close danger. At my feet was a lying girl who looked as if she was dying. She craned her neck to aim at me. I didn’t plan to give out my life just yet, so I turned around and ran. Unfortunately, the girl managed to grip my shoe with her teeth. She clenched them so subconsciously to hinder my departure. That was my favorite pair of shoes, but it was nothing without a pair. The ghastly strength of her teeth drags me back from the thoughts. It was a choice between living and shoes. Though all the shocks that morning, I didn’t lose my mind. I wish to stay alive. That was my only and ultimate ambition by then. I ditched the shoes, both of them, knowing that a single shoe could trouble me when sprinting. Now that the plan of obtaining sports equipment was proven infeasible, I made a swift switch to plan number two, which was to get the sticks, and from farthest to the right of floor two.

It didn’t take me too long to grab the wooden sticks. Five sticks in total were placed in a basket. I took away two, in case one got broken as I fought. The rest I left them for the upcoming lucky guys who would find themselves in an extremely advantageous position. They shall learn the benefits of being slightly selfish during a disaster.

Right next to the activity room was the nursing center, where I planned to get some vital medicines. As I got into the room, I saw the figure of a girl who was hiding under the bed on the other side of the curtain. It so happened that the bell knelled twelve times and it was too loud that she didn’t take note of my entry. I heard her holding on to a phone call. She put it on speakerphone so that the call was clearer. There were some disturbing background noises, but I could still recognize fragments of words. It was about the further instructions that we’d been waiting for and had yet to come. The speaker said in a panic. Apparently, he was in danger as well. The voice became distinct after a while. He promised to the girl, in the name of a father, that he had sent a military helicopter to our school. It would carry her to the most secure shelter. He asked the girl to stay away from the madmen who as he addressed ‘real zombies in life, nothing better than those in a doomsday movie’. ‘It all started from the mutated mosquitoes from the lab,’ he said in conspicuous grief, making his final speech, ‘my daughter, make sure to wear enough protection. I have sent two full sets of protective clothing; Stay away from the street lights at night. That’s where they gather; This isn’t the first day. It started months ago elsewhere. We locked the news down. Papa loves you, as always. My wish is that you stay safe. I will take care of mama and myself. It will take about one hour until the helicopter arrives. Do not take anyone with you, not even your best friends. You take all the water and food, only for you. Learn to be selfish my lovely daughter. That’s an important lesson. Papa…’ The call got hung up.

The farewell was meant to be a touching moment. I didn’t know how she felt, but I was indifferent. Her father was most likely a government official. They promised us more information, which we probably would never get. I sincerely hated these people. But as a father, I guessed he did a great job.

Only at the wrong timing…

I had to utilize that only chance to escape. I knew that the school resources were not sustainable. Though her father told her not to bring anyone, I had to take the ride, no matter what the manner was.

‘I heard it all,’ I broke into the room and asserted, ‘take me with you, or else…’

For a second I saw the girl’s expression freezing. Her eyes were wide open. A series of unconscious blinks emanated from her face and indicated to me that she was seized with terror. Soon she calmed down, sitting stiffly with a poker face. She was more determined than I thought she would be. ‘Would you take me to my father?’ questioned the girl, and it went beyond my expectation slightly, ‘it can’t be true that papa encounters any accident. He’s making another one of those jokes that fall flat. Take me to my dad. I can assure you whatever you want.’

It was pathetic that she refused to accept a cruel truth that her father was most likely gone. She was too innocent towards the end of the world. I could be a bad guy, and I wasn’t so honest. I agreed casually to take her to her father. The plan in my mind was to skip that part and go directly to the shelter with her. That was mercy from my point of view. Anyway, I made my promise, and that included taking her to the rooftop and waiting until the helicopter arrived.

The stairs were not far from the nursing center. I held her hand to ensure she wouldn’t escape. We ran all the way back to the third floor with 2 wooden sticks in my hand, 6 strips of bandage on my shoulder, a few vials of antibiotics and a lock with its key in my pocket. The situation upstairs was not yet as bleak. The doors were shut for all classrooms, leaving Henry and Peter to wander about the hallway. There wasn’t another route to the rooftop. That meant we had to run into the two insanities. I felt people’s cursing gaze at me when I went past each classroom. Soundless words kept pumping out of their mouths. I couldn’t care less. However, I did struggle a little with handling the case of Peter and Henry. Should I kill them with the sticks? Should I only knock them down? Should I cripple them? None of the ideas stood out. They used to be nice to me. Could there be a better solution? I got trapped in the thoughts.

I did not come to realize that the two were approaching us in big steps. The girl reacted faster. She snatched a stick and struck it at Henry’s face. His nose was deformed after the blow. It didn’t stop him. The girl then hit Peter heavily on the forehead. He fell after a few jolts. I thought that would work out better, nevertheless, it wasn't long before he was back on his feet. I was finally aware that I need to beat them up completely. The girl couldn’t handle the sticks anymore. She blamed me for being a coward. I had no time to explain to her my concerns. I was then in charge of protecting her and myself. With all the pain and regret, I raised my weapon above Henry and Peter. They looked puzzled. I gave them a few seconds before striking. That was it. Compassion no more.

Blood sputtered over the ground.

We went over the corpses and headed toward the rooftop.

After passing through a storage chamber, I saw the outside world again. It was still windy and raining, so I decided to sit inside the chamber and rest until the helicopter arrived. My hands were dyed red. To look at them was to bring back the bad memories of what had just happened. I felt a little guilty and sorry for my classmates, but a helicopter could never carry hundreds of people, not even dozens of them. People would kill each other to compete for a chance to survive. We would even be drawn into the fierce fights. We stood no chance. We were only given the option to leave selfishly, as we were about to do.

I left the girl indoors and went outside to the roof. While looking down, I saw the security and my classmates kept banging on the door. With that scene, I started to conceive of prospects for those who were stuck at school. They were abandoned like my poor classmate, and nobody would ever come to rescue them. I thought at the time that I was obliged to give them an option. I should offer them a warning and have them decide where to go. I needed to lock the chamber and make sure none of them would reach the rooftop. However, floor one had submerged - the only entrance and exit were occupied…

My mind underwent so many conflicts with itself. I tried to push myself toward the more rational side, but the inherent sentimentality that I got accustomed to eventually coerced me to comply with the ethical baseline. I believed that a fair chance should be offered to everyone so that they could at least apply their free will to pick on their destiny. I decided to tell them most of the information I know, including the source of the infection, how might the infected behave, where on our campus could people potentially find some valid tools, and never go to the first floor. I made up my mind to keep the whole matter of escaping with the helicopter a secret and prevent people from coming up to the third floor. Instead, I shall convince them to use the escape route that led directly to the school’s back door. It could be risky, I was sorry, but that was then the only existing and plausible solution that won’t interfere with my personal interest.

I told the girl that I needed to take my water bottle with me. It was in the locker. She seemed to acquiesce. So I went down.

The staircase of the third floor had long been sealed with a dust-laden door. Only a few people came to the rooftop over the past few years. I was a regular visitor who enjoyed spending my lunch time there in peace. I was steadfast that not many people besides me knew about that secluded liminal space. But by that time, before I was halfway to the door, it was open. The horrific sight brought my heart into my mouth.

A leading student burst the door open. After the leader, there were countless others. Some of them had visible bruises -- it was evident that the loathsome lunatics of the first floor had spread the danger to the entire school by then.

I couldn’t allow that many people to be on the roof and share the little chance to live after being divided up, especially given that half of them were infected and might turn into a threat to us at any time. Some of them were undergoing the process of corruption. Wails and mourning were everywhere. For a moment I could come up with no strategy to surmount the final test. It demanded I give up humanity to an extent for the sake of living. I saw a water pipe near me that extended to the outside and was connected to a water sink. I picked it up at once and told the girl to turn the water on to the maximum. I flushed the flood of people down from the stairs before they could even react, then turned back to the chamber and locked the door solidly. As I was finishing up the final procedure, I clapped my eyes at people who stared viciously at me. They had not yet lost their mind but were an equally big threat to me as either Peter or Henry. I fear the unnecessary contacts which would in turn draw out my sympathy, putting me in a dangerous place.

I instructed the girl to neglect the knocks on the door, though able to see their figures across the blurry glass. We together did the count down, until the bell knelled 13 times, and until some loud noises of propellers intruded into the chamber, echoing through each corner. We boarded.

I turned back to the familiar ground and my school that was drifting away from the horizon. It was bleak as hell. I sighed, then spoke to the girl,

‘We are safe, for now.’

Down to the Sunless Abyss

On the afternoon of October 11, 1917, I, as the immortal German Imperial lieutenant and commander of the submarine U-18, successfully completed the combat instructions issued by the Admiralty. Our submarine sank freighter Antique from Southampton to Liverpool, and we filmed the whole sinking of the enemy ship with soldiers hidden on the boat fleeing almost crazily. Antique was slammed into the water by the strong German spirit. Its stern was rising high. Our camera did not miss any detail from this. I did not leave off those Antique crew members who wanted to survive. With my command, Captain Cleft and the sailors on the U-18 quickly wiped out all the deserters. Our submarine left the surface afterwards and continued deep sea exploration.

At sunset on June 19th, u-18 floated up again. This time we found a male corpse of unknown origin on the deck. His body was in a very strange posture, and his right hand was firmly grasped on the railing. I would have to admit that this man is very handsome. He may be of an Italian or Greek origin but is sure to be one of the deserters from the Antique. The exclamation of a sailor interrupted my thought there. He was holding an object found from the corpse. Well, it was a human head statue with laurel shape on top. Upon closer inspection, I found it somewhat similar to the black-haired deserter. Captain Cleft on the side was also attracted by this strangely shaped ivory statue. To put it bluntly, this is a rare treasure with extraordinary aesthetic value. I threw it back to the sailor for seeing his obsession staring on the statue, and commanded to throw the black-haired male corpse back into the sea. At this moment, most of the crew reported that they experienced some ‘ridiculous’ hallucinations. Schmidt and Zimmer claimed that they saw the black-haired man's eyes suddenly opened up and he sneered at the two of them who were told to throw the corpse. The superstitious sailor Muller, a villager from Alsace-Lorrine, shouted that after the black-haired male’s body was sunk in the water, he immediately spreaded his limbs, hid under the waves, and swam south. Just when I was about to rectify the military discipline, an enemy destroyer appeared. Thanks to the excellence of U-18 and the distance between two sides, we disappeared without a trace in water. The circumstance had determined for us that to flow back up in a short time would be of no difference with suiciding. We started to stay in the sea, where all secrets are to be buried.

One day, we noticed an ocean current that did not exist on the chart approaching. It was intending to entrap U-18 to sail south. Just as I was hesitating whether to venture up and ask for help from a nearby German ship, a shocking bad news opened the prelude to the U-18 mutiny. The location of the accident was in the power house of the ship. As I arrived, the radio equipments were utterly paralyzed, and even thick pus like mud was leaking all the way to my leather shoe. Schmidt, the sailor who threw the corpse before, was found dead in the strangest and most violent way——his eyes almost pumped out of his eye sockets. It was evident that he wore a set of headphone and recorded in his notebook some weird inscriptions that appealed to me like cult symbols. No doubt that Schmidt was scared to death by something.

In the following week, weird things happened from time to time, which greatly frustrated our iron German spirit. U-18 was planned to stand by and intercept the passing British cruise ship, yet the US warship hovering overhead like a vulture has completely deprived all the left breathing space. The submarine could not flow upwards. We were left alone deviate from the messages of the empire. We now got trapped in this cold and closed underwater space, where there is no sound, no light, no family, no hope, but still our glory. Some crew members began to turn maniac. Like dying people, they groaned and wailed one after another, wandering everywhere. A hysterical sailor even begged me to throw the ivory statue back into the sea. This didn’t make any sense to me.  Sailor Muller was still the most annoying man. He kept talking about the submarine being “haunted”. Muller believed that he saw a group of gray and swollen water corpses outside the window. They floated into the cabin under the guidance of the black-haired man, staring at him with resentful eyes, and refused to leave. In the end, Muller's fear approached a critical point. In tears, with a pistol, he shot himself. After Muller committed suicide, Schmidt and Zimmer also disappeared inside the submarine. Captain Cleft and I searched up every corner of U-18 and failed to find their bodies. The lives of German soldiers were precious, and even ignorant and superstitious villagers like Muller should devote their limited lives to the immortal German Empire. For this reason, I confiscated and sealed up all the crew’s guns, leaving only two pistols for Captain Cleft and I, who had not yet fallen apart mentally. From that moment, all messages about U-18 being haunted were taboos in the military. If there were rumors to confuse the soldiers, all those who disturbed the military shall be executed.

Food supply had never been a problem. Although we had run out of bread and bear, we’d still have beef and pork. Chief Schneider cooked these meats with a special flavor. Six sailors roared, cursed, and almost smashed all the instruments and utensils in the cabin. They talked nonsense, saying that everything was to blame the curse of the ivory statue and the mysterious men. Captain Cleft, who had never seen a camp-bomb, was already stunned. What a bummer! I executed all six mad sailors with absolutely no dilemma, and commanded Cleft to throw their body to the sea via the pipes.

Say that the previous explosion had taken away U-18’s limbs, then this mutiny had certainly pierced its eyes. Those mad man destroyed all precision instruments and navigation equipment and made the current U-18 of no difference with a dying body. The increasingly presumptuous southward ocean current insisted on carrying us to an unknown sea. Over the next month, Captain Cleft and I survived with little electricity and oxygen. U-18 had lost the ability to float long before the explosion. Until that very moment, it had sunk to a depth where most marine life cannot survive. This is the chaotic graveyard of them all, and the foreseeable destination for Cleft and I. Strangely, the dolphins surrounding the submarine were still alive. I remembered watching a dolphin for two hours non-stop, and discovered that it had not been ventilated once.

It was possibly December 3, when Captain Cleft conflicted the ultimate failure against insanity. He stared at the ivory statue intently for several hours, talking about ridiculous mysteries about underwater civilization, and was confessing to something. May god bless all men who remained sane with the horrific truth.” Cleft invited me to go with him, and begged me to leave himself alone if I’m not going with him. I followed the searchlight towards the south days and nights. In a trance, I saw some temples shining with the glory of the ancient times, and they rushed to the bottom of the valley, on the steep slopes visible to the naked eye. The buildings are exquisite. Please forgive my poor language, because any human wording is a futile description of what I see. My gaze got fixed on a towering shrine, and those carved beams and paintings, all gorgeous enough to make literary scholars poor, all presenting grotesque and ineffable contents. Until tremor of the landing of U-18 awakened me from illusions, all my thoughts got turned back from this shrine that condensed the civilization of the exotic world. The resources were almost exhausted, and the iron will of the Germans, which I couldn’t feel less proud of, no longer defends me from the disturbances of the outside world. When the lasting match in the room faded away, the boundary between illusion and reality was blurred itself. But the moment I was about to pull the trigger, a dazzling spark plunged into the cabin without warning. It tore through the darkness in the depths of the ocean, and pulled me to the light. I recorded everything that happened after my boat sank Antique. I put this note of the Lost Temple of the Atlantic Ocean into a drift bottle, and after leaving U-18 forever, I threw it into the sea. The prophecy of the madman Cleft is still echoing in my ears, but I am not afraid at all. I know that the mania is just a fantasy before the oxygen runs out. Afterwards, I will put on a diving suit, walk out of the room to the sea, and I will gather the courage of all the immortal German martyrs to step against the illusionary glory.
