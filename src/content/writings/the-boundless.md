## Chapter One
A Bloody Christmas

Every child of my generation knew of Sam, son of the landlord Saggy William. They did two things to him across his short, sorrow filled life. First, they bashed him on his tenth birthday, Christmas Day, and broke three of his teeth. I was there, watching. His blood dripped, staining the Christmas night scarlet. He died young, but something of him has never ceased to haunt my ears: the sound of his pleading. As they beat him, he begged so pathetically for mercy.

But there was nothing to forgive.

He was beaten because his father had given him a bag of freshly baked cookies as a birthday gift.

And Sam had told them he would share.

But they wanted more.

Ten years later, at his funeral, the second thing was done, not only to him, but to his entire family. Music played. Old Saggy William wept. He was seventy years old, to us, a lifetime that felt like eternity. Sam was only seventeen. Whispers began that day, anonymous and venomous, claiming the father had secretly drained the son's vitality. The story was clearly false, yet it spread like a plague. No one knew who started it, but the rumor soon reached into every corner of our community, the grand churches where deities scorned to bless, the damp fish market smelling of blood at dusk, even the pristine edges of white calla lilies laid at his grave.

Two hours later, his body was incinerated in flame.

That same night, the Williams vanished, suicided. Not only Saggy William, but his wife and daughter, too.

They left behind what we saw as enormous wealth.

But the family was gone. No one remained to enjoy it.

So, it was divided evenly among the residents.

But they wanted more.

Young Sam never had the chance to have children of his own. The bloodstains on their mansion's carpet told the story of a family erased. If you ask me now who killed the Williams, I still cannot say for certain. They blamed Sam. They blamed his father. They said if not for Sam's death, the family would have prospered forever.

But a voice echoed in my mind, murmuring the truth:

It was us. It was me. We killed the Williams. I was part of it.

And now, so many years later, as snow begins to fall again, I remember. I remember the cookies, the envy, the violence. I remember how a gift could become a death sentence. I am older now, and the snow feels like a veil over that old blood. But some stains never wash away.

## Chapter Two
An Encounter in Snow

Yesterday was the second snowy season ever to come about in this world, or maybe it was today. I was only vaguely informed. The streets were dumped with a gelid velvet, all the usual landmarks and detached villas homogenized into a stream of white cascade. The flakes, the weather, all combined, were choking me. I was so overwhelmed with having to wander a long way to the bleak location where I often referred to as home. Deep in my mind, of course, I would love a caring car to rescue me from such an indifferent predicament and deliver me straight to the side of that antique stove. I envisioned a campfire ongoing.

That was when I bypassed the barely distinguishable auto shop, covered by a sheet of pale fabric. Closest to me was a Benz, a sedan, well equipped with air cons and a music player. I looked down at my hands, frozen stiff, conspicuously red. Gotta stop them from freezing dead. A pair of gloves would do, but a car, especially such a luxury, would sure bring me in no time to my home sweet home. The idea refuted itself in a pathetic few seconds. I was wearing a shabby anorak with patches all over it. Perceivable that the well dressed salesclerk, of season limited bad temper, would kick me out with no second glance. I didn't look such, nor was I remotely close to owning an extravagance like this. Also, I would've used, if I had it, this money on my family.

Still, the temptation arising from the idea of an Odyssey back home in a mechanized vehicle remained irresistible. I approached my head meticulously to the side window, where both the snow avalanche and curtain coverage were generous enough to not deprive my one and only chance to take a glimpse at the beauty. Steam diffused from every breath of mine, bleaching the frosty glass into a blurry transparency. Through the tiniest crack, across the thick plate glass, the car commenced to exist in my sight. Its design featured a perceivable delicacy. Nowhere else across the space would I be allowed to focus my sight upon. But almost subconsciously, I reached my hand into my pocket, reassuring the stark reality of its emptiness. Horribly, not a penny, and assume they always come in handy. It was alright, because I usually had no spare money. Today was just the same.

Every building brick of my house was constituted by bloody pennies in stacks, made from porting cargoes, not to mention the furniture and utensils. The memory of those truck driving days rushed back as a warning. I had driven for the merchants along the coastal route, hauling fish and scrap in the dead of night. I learned the roads. It was a skill from a life I had compartmentalized, a proof that I had once been useful in a way that mattered to someone.

The people within my hard-earned house devoured upon me and my coins and banknotes. Their existence rendered the car a fig. At least for me it is. Temptation beats me not.

I realized then that the Miss. salesman was out for a vigil. Most likely she was out to sweep me away. I embraced the hostility by default and stepped back bit by bit. She looked through my eyes, as if knowing all I had been thinking. I found her pupil much like an abyss, one that constantly attempted to condemn me for the guilt of approaching the unapproachable. The time of judgement had come.

My thought ceased at the moment. A liquified darkness seeped from my veins. My last consciousness reminded me that I became fainted.

While the penultimate sound echoed in mind:
'Who are you?'

## Chapter Three
The Office and The Offer

My name is Karl Joseph Anderson, an average man, no different from the others in this town, and I sat perfectly rigid at an unfamiliar desk.

How I came to be here was a mystery; my memory ended at the auto shop's door. Then I had a dream, a dream of me becoming a rich man. I remembered having a similar dream as a kid. But since then, like everyone else did, I surrendered the hunger for life, drowning in the stagnant waters of the present.

But this just couldn’t change the fact that I, too, had dreamed. Years ago, years past the initial few when I had no doubt about this whole matter being but a fiction, years before now, when the disbelief had chosen to repeat itself, I had faith in my father's distant paradise, never questioning his choice, waiting for him to return in a twilight stained with rose and gold again. Every time I saw that car in the shop, a machine that seemed to belong to some other, braver world, that old yearning would stir, echoing in the hollow of my chest. But then I would swallow the coarse, mold speckled bread, my only meal, its dry bitterness a rope pulling me back to earth. Where was this new world? If I could not at least fill my stomach, if my greatest joy remained sitting outside of a shop that never changed, dreaming about a door that never opened, then what were the feasts and fairy tales but cruel jokes?

We are all like this, I suppose. We clutch at what we cannot have. If our hands cannot hold it, we build it from words, branding the fantasy onto our hearts until it feels almost real, as if such wonders existed just beyond our reach. The day I understood this, chewing on that stale bread, was the day my childhood came to an end. The fairy tales were not true. I was not the chosen boy who would someday sail to a new world on the tides. Perhaps this role had been given to someone else. Or perhaps, from the very beginning, we are all handed a script for a play never to be staged.

So be it. Perhaps the world is just a stage, and life a beautiful lie. A finite, limited world that we nonetheless insist on seeing as boundless. I grew up, and my father remained the eternal dreamer. Or maybe he was the wise one, and I, having lost my guide, simply lost my way in the fear of what lay ahead, locking myself in a small, safe room of my own making.

Who was right, my father or I? I have searched for the answer a thousand times, always in vain. After he left, the question itself seemed to lose its meaning. The fire of my curiosity slowly drowned in a sea of indifference. Picking up these old thoughts again, I feel only a vague, sweet sorrow. Should I keep searching? Or should I let the thread of thought break here?

The woman in front offered me no time to hesitate. A slight twitch at the corner of her mouth had given away her impatience. Her gaze locked with mine, and I felt its weight. Her eyes were sharp, analytical, like those of an interrogator. I ran a hand through my hair, drew a sharp breath, a silent plea for a moment to gather myself. It was then I truly saw her, the crisp, professional attire, the ageless face. She was the sole attendant from the auto shop. The same one, same as then. A decade had passed, yet she was unchanged, a photograph in a world of fading memories. But I was not the same boy.

She had asked me who I was before I fell into darkness, but my mind was already adrift. Now, finding the chance, I opened my mouth to speak.

“Karl. I may call you that.”

“Yes, but,” my answer was strangled in its infancy, no, stolen, though I had never spoken my name.

“This isn't the first time you've been found loitering outside my shop, is it?” Her voice left no room for question. I met her stern expression, cleared my throat, and answered with a confidence I did not feel.

“No. It's not. I've been, curious about this place for a long time. I saw you as you are since I was a boy, and a long time has passed, long enough to make me who I am and unmake who I were, but you're always there, the same. Day and night. I remember one summer night, I can't recall the hour. The heat was thick enough to blur my mind, or perhaps the darkness was just too complete. It was late. I saw the lights were still on. I saw you inside. And then.”

“And then you don't remember.” Her lips curved into a smile that chilled my blood.

My composure cracked. She was right. That was precisely where the memory ended. I had forgotten much of my childhood, dismissing it as the dull residue of a mechanical life. But that night, that void was different. It was a conscious emptiness. And my instinct screamed that she held the key.

Her eyes were deep, abyssal. Holding her gaze made my skin crawl. It felt as though she could read the very fabric of my mind.

“You are wondering how I knew your name before your voice could form it. The question hangs between us, unspoken. There is no need for your reaction, I already see it taking shape within you. You had spoken with me yourself, a decade ago, in a voice not yet weathered by years.”

Silence was my only reply. I had felt the truth of it even as we spoke, a slow dawning recognition that settled cold in my veins. Yet I steady my breath and meet her gaze, a sailor facing the tide. This is the moment I visit what was lost, that hollow night, fever bright and blank as fresh snow.

“We had a conversation, between only you and me, of mice and man. You don't remember, and you cannot, not unless you had accepted what I offered, what we offered. Once, long ago, you turned away. Now you stand here, shaped by time. Tell me, has the 'crazy' dream of yours faded? That distant shore you once believed in, can you still see it?”

“I banished the illusions,” I respond, the words formal, yet heavy with all I do not speak. She is seeing it, the shadow of a boy who still dreams yet buried under years like fallen leaves.

“You have carried trauma through these year to my knowledge. The world is not exactly the gentle place you imagined as a child. Your father walked with stars in his eyes, and you inherited the shine. He set sail for a distant shore, and the sea kept him. You tried to follow, once, small and earnest as a prayer. But that tenderness, that boundless hope, it has been a weight upon you. To walk among those you once considered lost, to become one of them, it must have felt disappointing. But you did it so gracefully, as though returning to a skin you were always meant to wear. As though you never stood apart.”

Her voice slips like silk into old wounds. A fire stirs in me, raw, unrefined, and my reply trembles with a boy's defensiveness. “I never held myself above them. Never. We breathe the same air. We are one people.”

“A fraction of you stayed the little boy,” she says, her calm unnerving. With a deliberate grace, she gestures toward a small machine on the desk, a relic of captured time. That unsettling smile returns, a knife under disguise. “You still rush in like a storm. We've been through this before. I observed that your people were wounded, the wings behind their backs disabled. You were the one who named them 'losers.' You demanded an apology, and I gave you one. But what truly wounded you was the truth you already knew, that you did not quite belong. This shop called to you. Your returning, year after year, only confirms it.”

She leans forward, the air growing thin. “Why here? Why a place of dreams in a world of dust? Where do I come from? You asked me these things once. I answered then. I shall answer again. But first, ask yourself, why has no one else ever spoken of this place? Do you truly believe you alone have seen through the veil, seeing the Benz?”

Her voice drops to a whisper, like twilight settling. “They have all seen it. As children, they pressed their faces to the glass, just as you did. But unlike you, they turned away. To them, this place is a phantom: it does not fill their hands or still their hunger. So I have waited here, a decade deep, a stranger speaking to the silence. I had been waiting for others like you. I thought perhaps they, too, might have in interest in what I have to offer. But most never cross the threshold. And you, my dear, you've been here, you've been away, but had the snow not pushed you back into this reunion.”

A soft light kindles in her eyes, ancient and knowing. “I am glad you choose us again. Something, should we call it chance, or grace, has woven our paths together once more. A second meeting. Welcome home, friend.”

To my ears, it sounds like a riddle spun from starlight and shadow. Yet my mind, clear and still in this suspended moment, whispers one thing: The offer. What was the offer? That emptiness in my memory has ached for too long.

“And now,” she says, as though I had spoken the words aloud, “should we discuss about the offer?”

Of course. She had always been able to read what lay written in the silent language of my soul.

## Chapter Four
Wound

The woman straightened, her movement fluid as a dancer's. I found myself mirroring her unconsciously. We had been speaking for what felt like an eternity in that cramped office, its only window a high pane of glass through which no light is allowed to penetrate. Once again, as ten years prior, time had lost all meaning. I felt like a prisoner in a cell, bound by invisible chains.

She drifted toward the tape recorder she'd shown me earlier, her pace deliberate. I had sensed its significance the moment I awoke. This elegant machine stood out like a jewel in the dusty gloom of the room. Among the sparse furnishings, it was the only object that spoke of a world beyond our own.

“You must be curious to hear the voice of your former self,” she said, her tone lightly teasing. I didn't contradict her. It wasn't mere curiosity. The thought of being denied this truth felt like its own particular torture.

Her finger pressed the red button, and from the machine emerged a voice from another time.

“Consider the fairy tales to be real. Maybe just for a moment, would you? And you are welcomed hold this belief forever, because what I am about to tell you is: Yes, indeed. It's there; I've looked at it.” Those words, in a woman's voice I knew well, would forever be etched in my memory. Though brief, their impact shook me to my core. Maybe my father hadn't been so wrong after all. The boy I once was might have been right.

“But that world isn't built for everyone,” the recording continued. “You have the sensible eyes to project it, perhaps you belong with us. As for the others? They're either too blind or too cowardly to seek the doorway to that new world. They've disqualified themselves, condemned to remain in your impoverished community. They could take a visit here just about any day, speak with me on any day, cross over to the other side any day, but I know they never will. These people are molded. They accept their lot, having lost all will to go beyond it.”

A younger voice responded, my own, from a decade past, but softer, higher, with the uncertain rhythm of a child:

“But I know these people. They're my neighbors. Mr. Haggerty fixes shoes and sings. Miss Lina bakes bad bread but shares it. They're not bad. They're just, tired. And hungry. My dad said we should share good things. If there's a better world, why can't everyone go? It's not fair if only some people get to be happy.”

The woman gave a soft, mocking laugh. I understood her amusement now. That boy in the recording, I recognized his thoughts, but the idealism felt like something from another lifetime. I had become a man living in shadows; the bright certainty of childhood was too dazzling for my night accustomed eyes.

“Do you know the story of Prometheus?” Woman's voice in the record continued. “He stole fire from the gods and gave it to humanity, even taught them how to cheat in their sacrifices to the divine, all so that the hopeless souls could better fill their stomachs. Your tone may sound noble, but you're making his same mistake. You can't be a savior. Some things are made for certain people. To be shared amongst everyone would cause them to lose their meaning. Prometheus didn't end happily. The Olympian gods chained him to a rock where an eagle feasts on his liver for eternity. Later generations, seeing his defiance, came to associate him with Lucifer himself. Poor Prometheus, wanting to be the one, but ended up both failed and condemned.”

The boy's voice returned, confused: “I never heard that story. We don't have stories like that here.”

“Of course you haven't,” the woman's recorded voice replied smoothly. “Prometheus belongs to the New World. His tale is kept there, cherished, protected from those who would strip it of its meaning. But you know another story, don't you? The Odyssey. The long journey home. That one you've heard, because it serves a purpose here. It teaches patience, endurance, the hope of return. But Prometheus? He teaches rebellion, theft from the gods, ambition beyond one's station. That is not a lesson for this world.”

I listened, frozen. It made a terrible sense. We knew the Odyssey because it kept us dreaming of a return that never came. Prometheus was forbidden because he might inspire us to steal fire for ourselves.

The recording continued. “You're a little different from your neighbors in nature. Your hopeful heart, your father's legacy, they kept something within you that we hunt for.”

Her words struck deep, but I couldn't fully accept their meaning. This outsider spoke of my people as if they were hopeless cases. Saved? What saving? I remembered their resilience, the way life persisted even in our harshest moments. Even if they no longer dreamed of miracles, it didn't make them failures. A protest rose in my throat:

“We might be different. We don't have no stories like that. Our lives are painted in simpler colors. But we work, we struggle, we endure. However meager our existence may seem to you, you should never deny the dignity in that struggle.”

As I spoke, a strange realization dawned. The boy in the recording was giving voice to these very thoughts, these same convictions that still lived in my heart.

“My friend Mikey was digging worms off the earth. He called it good stuff. It ain’t good stuff, but it is valuable stuff. He liv with it. “

Then two voices overlapped, mine from the present and mine from the past:

“If I invited you to come over to our world, would you still refuse? Note that it's only you, and no one else.”

The true test had come. I needed to weigh every word, listen to every whisper of my heart. I wanted to say no, but then I remembered the luxury car in the showroom, the warmth of this office, and contrasted it with the biting cold outside. I thought of home, of our family gathered around the stove on rare nights when we had fuel, sharing stories in the flickering light. The memory warmed me, but it was incomplete. I recalled waking the next day to find the fire dead, the chill seeping into my bones until I fell ill. Lying on the cold floor, I saw my father's face; was I joining him at last? Then darkness took me.

When I woke, it was to the same shabby room, the same cobwebbed stove, the same chill air. There was a small bonfire. The last piece of wood nearly consumed. Reaching for its warmth, I burned my fingers. I could only watch helplessly as the final flame died. My wife approached, her face impassive. “You're awake?” she asked emotionlessly. The wound went deeper than any physical ailment.

Did the coldness rise from the dead fire? Yes and not only, I realized.

Later, I learned about the cost of my recovery. To pay for medicine and a few bundles of wood, my wife had sold my father's legacy. That included his diaries, his books, even manuscripts I'd never read. I was grateful I'd at least read his diary containing his dreams of a better world. That's almost all I had left of him. She'd also pawned a gold pocket watch from the attic, a treasure my father had never mentioned, something I'd been saving for my daughter's coming of age. The medicine felt bitter in my throat.

My gratitude soon curdled into resentment. I felt betrayed in my vulnerability, robbed of my most precious things when I couldn't defend them. Complex emotions tangled within me: guilt, disappointment, helplessness. A few days since, I left, a silent departure, leaving behind all savings I'd scraped together for years.

I don't know how long I've been wandering. Each day blends into the next in this perpetual twilight. The sun rarely shows itself anymore. Then came the snow, the first I'd seen outside of my father's books. They said snow once heralded abundance, but in my memory, it had never blessed our poor community. I trudged through the white landscape until I saw a building I recognized. The familiar car, the woman, and then,

This room.

I'd thought of returning home, but some final stubbornness told me that path was shuttered. I wandered about the same streets day after day. No one came looking for me. The thought brought both relief and sorrow. There's enough money to keep my family running for months, time enough for my wife and daughter to learn to live a life without me. My sickness had rendered me a burden. The man who took care of his family had become one who they had to care for. The shame of that transformation still burned.

A touch on my shoulder brought me back. The saleswoman stood over me. I wasn't in the snow after all. I was locked up in this warm chamber, with a cup of tea steaming before me. The relief of it felt palpable.

“So,” she said, “what is your answer?”

It seemed that the test wasn't quite over. Truthfully, after physically being through the harshness outside, I was forced to consider the New World a sanctuary. Plus, the price of admission didn't seem so steep.

“You said, your world opened its gate for me? And did I hear you right that it's a place where everyone could find their joy? My family and I, could we all?”

“No. You and you only. I've been clear.” Her tone allowed no negotiation. “If you want to come over to our side, first thing you have to do is to sever all ties to this one. You'll begin anew, in a happy life your people could barely dream of.”

For most in our community, the choice would be simple. They had little to lose. But I carried attachments. I just couldn't forget about my childhood friends, the adorable people who shared the same dream as I. My conscience whispered that I mustn't abandon them.

“They're just fellow travelers on your journey,” she countered, anticipating my thought. “Their part in your story is over.” She spoke of how concern for others should never outweigh one's own fulfillment. I wanted to argue, but the words felt hollow. I realized: people change. Did they still remember me? That I doubted.

“I'm not sure,” I said, avoiding her gaze. Unfamiliar voices rose within me, my conscience, yet different. I'd always put others first, but now a new voice spoke of self-preservation.

Besides them, I still had.

“You've changed,” she observed. “For the better. You're learning to put yourself first. That quality will serve you well in the New World. One grows simply through sacrificing their naivete. Congratulations. You're becoming one of us.”

“Damn right I have changed. I could be more pushed to make the decision you desire me to commit to, but maybe later. I need time to decide.”

She looked faintly troubled. “In principle, I could grant you sometime to say farewell. But speak of our offer to anyone, our agreement vanishes.”

“I promise I won't. Not a word. If I refuse your offer, I will be quiet. If anything adverse, do whatever you want with me. I need to wrestle with the choice. I yearn for a new life, but perhaps not at the cost of erasing who I've been. I won't pretend: you know I have a family. The man before you is both a dreamer and a father.”

“I see. I may not understand, but I respect your decision. Take the time you need. Just don't betray yourself in the end.”

She smiled dazzlingly, not laughed, smiled. Yet it was not the comforting sort of smile. It made me cringe. I kept reminding myself in silence that this whole encounter was bizarre, that was the word to use. Was this real life? Or was it just fantasy? All that I'd imagined was now coming into reality.

And, when I thought about it: had I ever received something of great benefit without being asked for anything in return?

It was a snowy day. The calendar said December. Tales spoke of an old man who brought gifts to children at a time called Christmas. I believed this experience was similar, yet clearly nuanced in certain regards. I was no kid anymore, she was a woman, and the gift had a price.

But surely, since that night, tale stories had become far more persuasive to me.

I started falling back into memories of reading about Santa, the giver of Christmas. I wasn't fully immersed yet. Something on the desk caught my attention. It was a pack of matches. Since my father's untimely departure, I'd acquired the habit of smoking when feeling upset, confused, or frustrated. But you always have to light them. Even this tiny act takes matchsticks, a serious cost to someone like me. With not a penny in my pocket, I'd kept my last pack of cigarettes unlit for as many days as my wandering life had lasted.

It was an animalistic desire. I didn't just want the match, I needed it. Smoke had permeated my blood. I took one from the bunch without saying anything, pretending it was natural. I understood it wasn't polite to take without asking. But I didn't want to ask. It would embarrass me. I tend to avoid smoking in front of ladies. An image of my daughter shaking her head, signaling a lack of favor, always surfaced in my mind.

I waited to see how she would respond to my misbehavior. To my surprise, no accusation was induced. It went even further beyond my expectation when she picked up a cigar from her pocket herself and swiftly, almost skillfully, lit it with a matchstick from the same pile I'd reached for. I very seldom witnessed people smoking in my community. It was a semi luxurious indulgence. And never, or so I thought, had I seen a woman smoke.

I stayed silent and seated, gazing at the matches.

“If you want, you can take them all with you. I have enough supply for, let me think, three days. I won't need all of them. A joy it might have been, but lethally addictive. The assumption is that sooner than that, your transfer will be completed. It's up to you, really. It's up to you.”

I'm no master at reading subtext, and at that moment, I wasn't fully aware of the meaning behind her words. All I knew was that I'd been granted a great gift.

“So, I'll come back in a few days with a clear answer. I don't know if you'll like it or not. I really don't.”

“Oh, dear. I would love it best. Enjoy the ride home.”

“I don't know about you. Tonight, since it's about a life changing matter, I'll have to set my feet on a trip to that place I chose to exile myself from. Can't tell you if I'm excited. Part of me is; part of me is pleading to stop.”

“Or will it be on your feet?”

She casually tossed a set of keys onto the table, even nudging them slightly in my direction.

“It's yours now. Drive it back when you've made your decision. Just come in next time. Park the car near the front gate.”

“How am I supposed to drive?” I asked, though a memory surfaced, brief and sharp: my hands on the steering wheel of a delivery truck years ago, back when I worked the docks. I had learned to drive out of necessity, moving goods for merchants who paid in food, not money. It was a skill I'd buried, like so much else.

“I understand that you're unlicensed,” she said, “but my knowledge also tells me that you used to drive a truck. This should be easier than that. Only three of you in your community have had the chance to do some driving. Coincidentally, you all are unlicensed truck drivers.”

“Even that you know?”

“I wouldn't lie about the depth of my investigation. I also wouldn't have this piece of information planted in my head if I weren't the one who arranged the trucks.”

I left, saying no more. A feeling of quiet rejoicing bloomed inside me. A lucky chap earned himself two unexpected presents in snowy December. With a booming noise, the engine started.

And so began my Odyssey back home in the delicate, mechanized vehicle.

## Chapter Five
The Old Man and the Sea

The vibration of the car, the purr of its engine, these sensations were alien yet familiar. My hands remembered the weight of a steering wheel, the resistance of gears. They also felt the smooth leather of the seat, the warmth from the vents, all of which contrasted so violently with the world outside. My mind began to drift. It drifted past the snow, past the shop, past the office. My car stagnant; I had yet to leave, but I am thinking about leaving my family. I will most certainly escape. My children would stay, and I would be gone. I stayed and he was gone. Now it was my turn.

How similar this felt: decades ago, I was my daughter, standing alone only to witness my father's departure.

To my child's mind, Father was a pilgrim to the tides, journeying to the sea each dawn. He would tell me that the ocean's breath was the very essence of liberty, that to walk into its embrace was to step toward the shores of a distant, promised freedom. I was too young then to decipher the melancholy poetry of his words, or to understand why one should yearn for such an abstraction. How blissfully bounded my world was, contained within the small horizon of his return?

He migrated always in the fragile light of dawn, while I still swam in the currents of sleep, his figure vanishing into the immense, grey longing that called to so many. And he would return as evening bled into dusk, when the final rose gold stain of sunset dissolved into the waiting stars, and the heavens mirrored the vast, dark water. Then, his steadfast silhouette would gradually emerge from the gloom at the door of our seaside home. Year upon year, day after day, without exception.

But there came a winter of a cold so sharp that it seemed to crystallize the very air. On a particular morning, I rose early and followed him to the water's edge. He bent down and chose a shell from the wet sand, a small, hard pearl of the ocean, and pressed it into my palm. He murmured words to me then, a secret for me to keep. That shell has never left me; it has traveled in my pocket through the swift, silent flight of ten years. Time has bleached its colors, but my father's words have only grown brighter, polished by memory to a luminous clarity, an eternal inscription upon my soul.

“Across the sea,” he whispered, “lies a new world, fresh and brave, and free.”

Then he stepped forward. One, and then another. The shy waves kissed his feet, then the rising water took his legs, his waist. His form began to waver, becoming a ghost, a delicate sketch erased by the thickening mist. He walked on, and on, until the grey curtain swallowed him whole. I knew he was only a few arm's lengths away, and this thought filled me with a quiet peace. I made no sound, but sat on the cool sand in the nascent light, waiting, waiting, trusting I would soon repeat the tender, familiar liturgy of our lives:

“Welcome home, pop.”

I did not know that the moment had our fates forever untwined. The short distance my small arm could have spanned was stretched by the slow, cruel hands of the years into an impossible chasm, an endless expanse I could never cross.

From that day, between my father and me, there came to lie a long, long road, paved with the silent stones of Time.

The memory faded, leaving the faint taste of salt on my lips. I rested inside the car, my shell. It was different kind of vessel, carrying me not across water, but through snow, toward a different kind of judgement.

## Chapter Six
A Little World On a Christmas Night

I was imagining how I would make move the luxury. In fact, I thought about it, probably three, maybe countless times. Step on the gas was my dream come true. My heart had been set ablaze. With every touch of the steering wheel, the joy renews itself. The air conditioning was turned to its maximum, yet it couldn't begin to cool the fire of my exhilaration. A kind of fanatic, perhaps even lunatic, euphoria burned within me, one I can only assume stemmed from the sensation of a dream fulfilled, or from the primal satisfaction of desire indulged and greed fulfilled.

I am the only one to receive a gift on this Christmas.

Yet this may not be the blessing it appears to be. It reminds me of that bloody Christmas I witnessed as a child, standing helplessly on the sidelines. Back then, we children would share cookies to mark the occasion. Their taste was profoundly awful, the kind that offended the senses. Some even bore empires of mold. We laughed at the one who drew the worst piece, its surface more green than brown. We seethed at those who received the prize: an unbroken, freshly baked delicacy. We fought, we traded, we schemed, every trick in our small hearts to swindle the finest cookie from its owner. Sometimes I saw my peers strike one another. I knew, even then, it wasn't truly about winning.

It was about breaking the beautiful cookies, so that no one could have what others could not.

You wouldn't exactly call it egalitarianism, but it was something darkly similar.

In dark would the kids be whispering,

“You turn!”

I chose the most obscure route home, hoping to evade every familiar face, to slip back unseen in the vehicle of dreams. Yet reality defied me. I recognized nearly half the pedestrians I came across; I could only hope they didn't recognize me in return. My head was hot with triumph, but my heart was cold with the memory of Sam's curtain call. I have seen the infinite depth of human greed. If one receives a cookie, all must have a bite, not out of fairness, but from the jealousy toward the chosen few, and the resentment at not being chosen themselves. In time, neighbors who put on smiley faces each morning become faceless green-eyed monsters. They will tear down what others have, if only to ensure no one rises above.

My car, this blessing, cannot be divided. But the brutes can dismantle it, into cogs, wheels, seats, fragments worth less than the whole. When laying hands on what is mine, they could temporarily soothe their envy, that I learned. It isn't achievement they seek, it's the satisfaction of ensuring I, too, end with nothing.

They are always satisfied with such an outcome.

My thoughts sank deep into this reflection on human nature, so deep I hardly noticed my journey nearing its end. But somewhere in that trance, I saw him, a man, standing directly in my path. He moved sluggishly toward the car. I had no choice but to stop since the street is too narrow for a car and a man to pass simultaneously.

Not only had my car wheels stopped for him, but also my wheel of fate, the peaceful Christmas night carrying so much euphoria. I shall never forget about tonight, when I became the Williams.
