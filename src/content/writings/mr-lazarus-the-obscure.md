A slow, almost imperceptible shift had thickened the air around William, so palpable even someone as obtuse as he could sense it. The once-bustling Instagram group had fallen silent, buried under an invisible avalanche. The timing felt unpleasant, portentous, and yet disguised as coincidence. This date had been marked as special, and only recently. Surely everyone remembered: the clever, forthright, brave hearts among them couldn’t have forgotten the unbearable weight that had settled over them this time last year. It was a silent burden, the kind that demands a lifetime to carry.

One conviction anchored William: the common thread among all those poker faces was deceit. They were hiding something from him. While storm clouds gathered on the horizon, he remained isolated under a solitary patch of blue, a fabricated sky, his own personal Truman Show. Once again, he had been cast as the stranger, and the bitterness of the role had only doubled.

He was also aware—or believed he was—that they bore no intention of ostracism. Using the holiday as a pretext, he reached out to the most discerning of them: Bruce. His acceptance was immediate. Days later, they sat in a café nestled inside an antique Mediterranean-style building.

“So, old chap, how have you been lately?” William began tentatively.

“Getting by, barely,” Bruce replied with an air of nonchalance that swiftly dissolved with his next words. “But it does feel like something’s missing.”

He had walked right into William’s line of fire. That sense of absence was precisely what William clung to; he knew exactly what he lacked. His own curiosity was what seemed to have created this void in Bruce. Despite six years under one roof, William still couldn’t comprehend the workings of that genius mind that embraced entire biomes. Surely even Bruce’s composed nature couldn’t warrant indifference, not when the person in question was so inextricably linked to them both. They had been each other’s crutch, stumbling out of childhood, only to part ways at the crossroads of adulthood.

“Totally agree,” William ventured, then carefully steered the conversation. “What do you suppose it is?” With that, he began leading Bruce into his carefully prepared trap, where the whole truth would have to be divulged.

“What could it have been?” Bruce propped his chin on his hand, seemingly lost in thought. Then he suddenly slammed the table and shot up from his seat.

“Eureka!”

“Oh man, I know it.”
“And it couldn’t have been anything else.”

They exclaimed in near unison.

“The funeral.” / “The game!”

Baffled stares turned toward them from surrounding tables. William saw his own reflection in their puzzled eyes. Anxious to avoid a scene, he took Bruce’s hand and pulled him back down, lowering his voice for the second round of questioning.

“What are you talking about?”

“What? Don’t be such a buzzkill. I know the game sold terribly and the studio’s probably going under. But calling it a funeral? That’s a bit much. You artists are all the same, always so dramatic. No offense, but I prefer the folks in the lab, the ones who actually get their hands dirty.”

William’s anger threatened to overflow. The disdain for his character was one thing, but “dramatic” felt like a violation. He was trying to discuss the matter, that matter, a matter of life and death. Bruce couldn’t be blind to it; he was deliberately evading and mocking him. A vein throbbed at William’s temple. Wordlessly, he gripped Bruce’s hand and pulled him out of the café. “Your change,” Bruce muttered, glancing back at the crumpled hundred-dollar bill on the counter.

William pulled him into the deserted alley beside the café and unleashed his rebuke in a tone designed to linger near his ears all day.

“Dramatic? Is that what you think he is all about? Criticize me all you want! That only wounds my pride, and I’ll take it. But what right do you have to speak of him that way? He was damn important to us both!”

“You’re the one who started it! What have you been going on about?” Bruce’s face showed only a furrowed brow and utter bewilderment. “The hell is ‘he’? What am I supposed to talk about? I don’t get it.”

“You!” William stepped back, leveling his index finger like a pistol. This was his final ultimatum.

“Stop escaping. Playing the fool won’t make this go away. He is gone, and it’s time for us to face it.”

“I…”

“What are you going to do?” William cut him off. “Keep up this charade until the anniversary passes? Then let day follow day, year follow year, until the memory sinks into the mud and it’s as if he never was? Kevin. August 17, 2024, just at daybreak, he passed alone in his bed, still a boy. At the simple funeral that followed, we were the only two who didn’t show up. The excuse of being abroad… never a good one. I’ve been a man underground ever since. A sick man… a spiteful man. The despicable type, a coward. I’ll always regret it. And now, knowing it’s terribly late, with the proper service happening this Sunday… I have to be there… You’ll be with me, won’t you?”

Bruce stared, mouth agape, wearing an ineffable expression—a mix of helplessness, confusion, resistance, even panic. They held that pose for what felt like an eternity, until a gust of wind sent a leaf skittering against Bruce’s cheek. Only then did he regain his voice.

“William, have you been under too much stress lately? Forming hallucinations? You haven’t been… seeing things, have you? Because about all that, you seem to have gotten a few key facts wrong.”

“Impossible.” William pinched his own arm; the pain was acute and authentic. This was no daydream, but it felt utterly unhinged from reality.

“Everyone was there, remember, every single person. You wept until you were hollow, even knelt down. As for me, I didn’t cry. It was too much to process then. If you’re here to hold me accountable for that, then vent all you want. Direct your anger however. I’ll try to be accommodating for once. And, most crucially…”

“Most crucially?”

“It’s just the one thing impossible to forget!”

“And I haven’t forgotten. Stop talking nonsense. Whether you cried is irrelevant. You weren’t even there! I was the one who cried, my tears drying on the other side of the world. I was not present. What you’re saying now… I don’t understand, I can’t comprehend…”

William realized he was sobbing, exposing his fragile self to the most unreliable of men.

“Kevin is no dweller of the past. He came back.”

“Came back?”

“He’s been resurrected. Don’t you remember?”

“He’s been resurrected?”

“Yeah. You were the one laughing the hardest. He was laughing with you.”

“Bruce, I thought you were calm, rational. I never expected you to also…”

“You’re probably mistaking me.” Bruce touched the bridge of his nose. “If I consider you a patient suffering from stress-induced amnesia, maybe affected by the Mandela Effect, then my words might be hard to grasp. Simply put, he is resurrected, in the most literal sense.”

“How?”

“Like the famous, maybe infamous, Mr. Lazarus who rose from the earth. First a hand emerged, then a foot. He should have been shouting underground, but we above could barely hear. Tony’s got good ears. He caught the sound and gathered people to dig him out. You were at the forefront. You pulled his head from the soil, like pulling a radish. Need me to demonstrate?”

“Bruce.” William paused, swallowing hard. “The dead do not come back to life. You’re a biologist. You know this. The dead do not return.”

“It’s what you may call a miracle. A divine miracle.”

“Nietzsche claimed God is dead. I don’t take it word for word, but I see his point. Miracles are like dyeing clear water wine-red, the taste unchanged. That’s the so-called miracle. People don’t come back. If they did, World Wars would repeat seventeen times over.”

“A miracle is something with a one-in-eight-billion probability.”

“So if I took this brick and struck you down, would I see you tomorrow?”

“Biologically, the chance is low. Please don’t. Humans are fragile.”

“Then stop messing with me. There are no miracles. Or tell me, what is a miracle? Scientifically, physically, theologically, what is it? Why would one descend upon us?”

“Everyone experiences a miracle once. Your existence is itself a miracle. To be the one victor among billions of sperm…”

“Enough! I don’t want that. That’s not justification. I don’t need consolation telling me my existence is a miracle. That’s metaphysics dodging science. It has no practical meaning and won’t resolve my confusion.”

“Then consider quantum physics. The Heisenberg uncertainty principle: Δx · Δp ≥ ℏ/2. Δ represents uncertainty. Change itself is the miracle.”

“So, can the dead return?”

“An excellent question. Existing theories can’t provide a definitive answer.” A faint, childlike smile flashed at Bruce’s mouth, impossible to doubt.

“But he has returned. That’s the established fact. The miracle. It can’t be explained by common sense or science.”

“Unless I see the mark of the nails in his hands, and place my finger where the nails were, I will not believe.”

William could bear no more.

Liar.
Coward.
This isn’t real life.

An observer might have seen a man fleeing another at the speed of light. Between him and the nauseating, straight-faced deceiver stretched the same distance as between mice and men. Farther and farther, until the other’s shadow vanished, and the slanting rays of dusk fell upon him relentlessly.

Bruce vanished from William’s world, perhaps forever. Yet in the penultimate second before time seemed to still, a reminder, surpassing and disregarding the traffic signals between them, cutting through the dimness, caught up to him.

“If you want to see for yourself, find him in the church this weekend. Don’t forget, you too received the invitation.”

Invitation?

“At the formal service, everyone will inlay the sight of his resurrection into their corneas. After that, resurrection will be myth no more.”

A streetcar passed, lyrics drifting through the air:

Is this the real life, is this just fantasy? Caught in a landslide, no escape from reality…

….

Hands covered William’s eyes.

“Guess who?” A lively, somewhat innocent voice. It was Tony.

It was Saturday. William had proactively asked Tony to visit the science museum. They shared little common interest in science, but that didn’t matter; he was there for another purpose.

After a night of deep contemplation, his mind had summarized yesterday’s bewildering conversation. Bruce was simply too tired; he needed rest. William had been too agitated. Perhaps he should compromise, forgive him. Or perhaps not.

He reflected on existence. Existence is external, social. He didn’t subscribe to “I think, therefore I am.” Existence requires third-party confirmation; death isn’t the technical end—oblivion is.

But the state of being alive, living flesh and consciousness, differs from the one-dimensional impression left in another’s mind. The living and the memorized are separated by a definitive line.

Without doubt, Kevin held eternal significance for William, but he had become the latter, unlikely to return to the former. Bruce thought otherwise. They stood on two sides of the same mirror.

Thus, a third party was needed, either to reconcile, or to destroy the other’s belief. Either would suffice. William needed only an answer.

After touring most of the museum, passing the gigantic lunar model, they reached the final room’s entrance.

William turned and gazed at the dated mimicry of the moon, wondering if human attainment of it was ever real. Did they discover anything there? More strange thoughts surfaced. He had fantasized about the moon since childhood. Where do the dead go? Heaven? The moon? Perhaps the moon is heaven. He hoped that within the obscure darkness of its far side, countless souls lay hidden, gazing at the once-familiar blue planet, yearning to return.

If so, why did he alone come back? Is death not a full stop, but a rewind button? Around this time last year, William had clearly heard of his death: cleansed by flame, turned to dust. Could hearing be deceptive?

He had been taught that seeing is believing. Yet in the farewell ceremony filled with old affections, he had become the stranger. He had not witnessed Kevin’s death with his own eyes.

The final room, almost empty, was the interrogation space he had prepared for the second participant. With the aid of a lie detector, he would discover whether it was his dear friend who had perished; or his own perception of reality.

Having come this far, he yearned for the former. And feared the latter.

Had he been suspending terrifying thoughts, surviving remorselessly? His objection towards Bruce could be escapism. He was afraid, merely observing, unable to accept Kevin’s departure, nor brave enough to embrace a fact beyond his cognition.

Upon hearing the news of Kevin’s return, his primal response was denial. Fear, guilt, shame over his absence, powerlessness as a disloyal onlooker. Perhaps he didn’t truly want Kevin back—because it meant too much. Tearing down a carefully constructed cage, releasing a clipped bird into the sky. It also meant becoming Mrs. Lazarus, the wife of the resurrected. Years ago, he had read Carol Ann Duffy’s poem from her perspective, a reimagining of the biblical Lazarus story. Mrs. Lazarus, having nearly healed from grief, witnesses her husband’s rebirth and is deeply troubled. Years later, William felt sympathy for her. To see someone familiar metamorphose into an existence beyond reason, everything turning unfamiliar. Such an Unheimlich experience evokes rejection, not acceptance.

But still, as a friend, he knew Tony would tell him the truth now.

You will tell me he’s back. Is that affirmative?

“Yes, he is.” Not a trace of doubt.

The lights came on.

He’s back.

Late at night, the church blazed with light. Beams scattered, yet all converged upon a single point.

A massive coffin rested on the altar, and within it, the one William held dear.

He missed him. He wanted to see his face, feel the warmth of his palms, touch him so he would no longer be beyond reach. They would know each other anew. This time, William would not look away.

The ceremony began. Sacred hymns filled the air as everyone offered silent prayers.

At the music’s crescendo, the coffin lid was lifted. Inside lay a handsome young man, face radiant with vitality. Like Tennyson’s Lady of Shalott, he lay recumbent, yet exuded the breath of life.

The congregation gazed up. Tony, Bruce, and William, in that moment, shared the same anticipation.

The priest finished the eulogy, marking the symbolic end. But suddenly, an aurora shattered the night sky, illuminating the giant cross and the coffin, painting everything in brilliant, resplendent light.

It was nothing short of a miracle.

They called in a loud voice, “Kevin, come out!” The dead man came out, his hands and feet wrapped with strips of linen, and a cloth around his face.

He is raised from the Dead.

They raised with the Dead.

If divinity could be made manifest, this was it. Through Baroque stained glass, a form gradually merged with Kevin’s, surrounded by dazzling radiance. With no visible support, he rose from the coffin. William saw the incredulous expressions below; some jaws had dropped. He tried to calm himself, forcing back tears, adopting a poker face he had never mastered. He shouldn’t appear too surprised; they had told him this was promised. Like Arthur returning from Avalon, they had always known he wouldn’t leave them.

Then—

“Bang.”

Followed by: “Bang, bang, bang.”

Scarlet surged like a vortex.

A distant trigger pulled, setting fate in motion. The bullet traveled a near-straight trajectory, piercing two parallel forms, the Divine, and his heart, simultaneously, like the Lance of Longinus.

Stained glass showed only monotone color. First, a terrifying red, then the world eaten by gray. Blood-stained roses in the coffin seemed the Flowers of Evil.

A newborn life taken, with no four-foot box here, only deadened hearts.

He had just risen, only to fall with a clatter. Like an abortive sun born in morning and perished by night, the returning one brought meteor-like brilliance, vanishing in a blink.

Bruce and Tony rushed forward. Some strange force held William back, rooting him to the defiled white-silk path. More transcendent than a dream, heavier than reality, this moment overwhelmed him. Trembling, he leaned forward; his knees kissed the earth. Then, a churning in his stomach, pungent mucus covering white and red, adding grotesque yellow to his vision.

“Hang in there, Kevin! Don’t you know how long we’ve waited? Don’t leave us again!” Bruce screamed heartrendingly. Tony compressed the chest, launching a final revolt against Death. It was futile. The one said to have cheated death like Sisyphus couldn’t escape again.

The flock scattered from the terror attack. No one sought the shooter or motive. The outcome was sealed; questioning seemed pointless, and horrifying.

The vast church held only the three of them. William finally moved, sitting cross-legged before the body. Tony averted his gaze; Bruce rushed out searching for the killer, entrusting William with the final moments, a reluctant acquiescence. If lack of witness, their absence at the first funeral, had fostered belief in resurrection, this searingly vivid second funeral dashed them back to reality. They say a cat has nine lives; today William learned a man can have two. But curiosity killed the cat, and man is doomed all the same.

He still desired to see the face, the face wrapped in cloth. Only the head and neck remained relatively intact. Carefully, he lifted the veil.

That is… that is…

For months, William had hidden it from them—a reversal since this bizarre incident began. He had offered them a chance to see Kevin one last time together, a gesture meant to convey a message. They declined, self-accusing. They wouldn’t have guessed what lay beneath: not their silenced beloved, but a deathly visage inducing primal dread. They would never imagine it; William hadn’t either. It was as impossible as the dead returning, yet eerier.

He had encountered his own death at his funeral.

Impossible to mistake. The facial features, the mole’s position, the ear’s shape. Oddest of all, the chest, seemingly penetrated, still held a pulsation, a tell-tale heart.

The bullet had missed by millimeters.

A blood-stained note slipped from the suit’s pocket into William’s hand. With his back to the others, he unfolded the paper. A quirky line stared back:

Visit the place that shouldn’t be.

Immediately, he thought: the unknowable cannot be visited. But memory fragments suggested a possible location.

They had said Kevin returned long ago. Tony had confirmed it. If the life in the church wasn’t his important man, then who had they encountered? It was him. But William knelt perfectly fine on the ground, facing another who supposedly was him. Only slight differences: a few more wrinkles, a thinner build.

They also said Kevin had lived in a Naples seaside house for the past year. His friend and that location had never crossed paths. William inferred the choice was somewhat “his” own. He did hold special affection for Naples; it symbolized romanticism. If he was indeed himself, then Naples might be the place that shouldn’t be.

Had he foreseen William would read the note? Perhaps only William would know the true location. Tony and Bruce knew of it but would miss the oblique connection. “He” wanted to tell himself something, through an exclusive channel.

These thoughts cycled as William flew to the Apennine Peninsula. Boarding the coastal sightseeing train, he felt paralyzed by the detective game. The note was a curse, or the encounter itself was. In Shintoism, meeting one’s doppelgänger is an ill omen.

The place that shouldn’t be was at 1027 Marina Street, a remote corner. A medieval-style villa stood there, defying Kevin’s taste for postmodern architecture. The entrance was ajar, sparing William from scaling walls. Cautiously, he pushed open the door, greeted by sandalwood scent. The door led to a reception room furnished with antiques. A mirror sat opposite the main door. The fireplace held charcoal, deep black from long disuse. Dust everywhere triggered his allergies, sneezes uncontrollable. It felt familiar. It was like the first time he moved into the high school dormitory, standing with Bruce and Kevin before the dorm door. William in the middle, Bruce right, Kevin left. Fear and resistance to growing up made them hesitate. Finally, Kevin stepped forward, pushed the door open. A dust cloud flew out; they sneezed. Kevin’s hair turned gray from dust mites. They laughed joyfully, childlike, calling him a little old man. Now, thinking back, the past was buried by dust, and the season of their youth.

A spiral staircase led to the second floor. Here, the impression was different: more vibrant. The coffee table held uncleared cups and dishes. William’s fastidiousness made him arrange them neatly; then, subconsciously, he took them to the sink. In the long years ahead, no one would care if they were clean. This was a place that should never have been. But he found no reason not to cherish what was before him. Had he known this principle earlier, could he have spent more time with him? Would that have brought redemption, or its opposite?

Darkness fell. Daylight traces vanished; firelight became the sole companion. His exploration neared its end. In a second-floor bedroom full of childlike whimsy, he found what might unlock the mystery: a diary titled Future Log, yellowed pages encased in an overly luxurious leather cover adorned with emeralds—a work of art displaced in this obscure mansion.

Who would use such a pamphlet as a diary? William wouldn’t dare touch it. If it were him…

The booklet was empty as a snow-covered ranch in winter.

He had inspected most suspicious objects; this was his final task. Opening it, he hoped for a comprehensive story. How naive.

He considered taking it for study, but an inner voice warned of personal appropriation. Research was a self-serving justification. After an internal struggle, he decided to respect the deceased and reluctantly held the diary.

Then, a gust blew in; pages flipped frantically, as if willful. A blinding ray appeared, and crooked strokes began on the first page, intertwining into barely legible words.

Write down what you wish to know.

So it commanded.

Countless strange encounters had inured William’s nerves; he thought nothing could perturb him further, but this was different. Since Kevin hadn’t truly returned, he hadn’t witnessed Bruce’s miracle. The dead “him” could be an imposter, conspiring with Bruce and Tony to trick him. However absurd, it held plausibility. These days felt like an immersive dream.

Yet the genuinely impossible now lay before him, magical realism without the realism. A Grimoire, cradled in his hands.

He took out his pen and obeyed.

Who was dead in the church?

You are the dead; we are the dead.

You are the future. All of us the dwellers of the future.

William’s mind disarrayed.

Words continued:

This is not magic; it is technology. When humanity steps beyond its comfort zone into a Brave New World, they crown it supernatural. What you see is decades of scientific effort.

A slightly overweight middle-aged man in a white lab coat appeared on the other end of the diary, sitting in claustrophobic darkness, exiled from sunlight, a resident of the kingdom of shadows. An electronic screen in his palm emitted a faint artificial blue glow, the only light source.

William continued.

“Did Kevin truly come back to life? With future technology?”

An unexpected answer:

Did Jesus truly come back to life at some point?

Rhetorical.

William was speechless. The diary produced another inquiry:

Do you believe in the power of belief?

If you believe Big Brother existed, that Oceania is at war with Eurasia and allied with Eastasia, then Kevin likely returned. The commons see him not as a relic, but living, transcending humanity.

“Answer my question, please.”

The re-creation of physical bodies is unattainable, now and forever.

“So.”

But the future people desire his resurrection more than you. His story, a modern Mr. Lazarus, became spiritual sustenance. An echo chamber formed; belief scattered, collided, reaffirmed. In collective cognition, Kevin is the revitalized. The physical embodiment of remembrance has a unified name…

“The God.” / God

In the distant future, the world falls into prolonged chaos. In 2044, quantum physics births the Observant Camera Obscura, glimpsing “possibilities.” People saw a world on the brink, yet restoring order. Through two researchers, an elixir countering nuclear genetic pollution was developed, preventing massive population reduction…

“So they were saviors.”

But in reality, by 2044, four years after the nuclear threat, the elixir wasn’t released. Not until 2048 did it become available.

“Was the delay the culprit?”

It caused further decrease, but wasn’t the main issue. Even in 2048, few had access.

“The privileged.”

Not privileged, but a faith-bound group. A new religion’s leader developed the elixir, distributing it freely to pious followers. The teaching: belief. Unconditional belief that the leader, publicly known as “the companion”, had died and returned, entrusted by God to save the world. He who perished and revived was your companion. The name unmentioned.

“Kev.”

Messages plunged William into convulsion. The last words conflicted with earlier impossibility, puzzling him. A million questions swirled, but he wrote the crucial one:

“Was it Bruce who developed the elixir?”

William, have you heard of the butterfly effect?

“A butterfly flaps its wings in the Amazon, causing a tornado in Texas weeks later. Unassociated, yet inevitable causality.”

Exactly. The Observant Camera Obscura observes “possibilities.” Many equate 99.9999% with 100%. The remaining chance dominating is no less than a miracle.

“Miracle. That word has torn me apart. Please, what is a miracle? What does it have to do with Kevin, his resurrection and funeral?”

A miracle is: among one million parallel universes observed, in inevitable future decay and reconstruction, only one yielded annihilation without recovery. The reason: replacement of one researcher in the duo. Bruce becoming the savior is preordained. The other, due to untimely death on August 17, 2024, was replaced by a companion—

Yes, on this world line alone, a certain boy fell into eternal sleep, and you, his friend, the future “companion,” became the second half.

“That led to…”

In 2044, Bruce developed the elixir. But he delayed release, citing “needing to verify a hypothesis.”

After theological and philosophical research, he concluded: collective remembrance and attached importance reconstruct existential presence. Resurrection, in a sense. The experiment required artificially creating irreplaceability, entrusting it to someone to sublimate into eternal existence. On August 17, 2044, 20 years after Kevin’s death, he proposed founding a religion, using control of the elixir to endorse Kevin, manipulating “the companion” to make him irreplaceable—

God, as the ultimate gift in his memory.

The man in the diary was one of the three present when William proposed attending the formal funeral together, the grown-up Tony, sighing after adjusting ill-fitting glasses.

“Love is, by the end, the most twisted curse.”

“Then, the ‘me’ who died in the church, was that the Companion ‘me’? Who killed ‘me’? How and why?”

As the Companion, you wore a mask, repeating daily: “Join us, for I am the ‘Companion’. “

Witnessing cruelties from a God-like perspective, your heart grew insensitive. Proselytizing in Naples, you saw three children before a nearly destroyed villa—skin and bones, covered in soot, needing to gamble for food and water. At the doorstep, they stood side-by-side.

Memory fragments surfaced. William saw himself lost before a certain corridor, familiar yet abnormal.

In that memory, a child version stood alone before the door, beside two empty spaces and infinite hollowness.

Due to dangers inside. Killers, ruffians, radiation, none would act first. After time, the left boy rushed to open the door… Dust flew into their faces, making him look masked. They laughed joyfully, childlike. Inside was safe. Upstairs, a complete set of furniture stood meticulously cleaned, in apocalyptic times.

Watching them, you touched your mask’s left side. Prickly, uncomfortable. For the first time in years, you felt suffocation. You removed it, breathing greedily. Suddenly, darkness; consciousness transferred elsewhere.

There, you encountered familiar people and things. They didn’t respond; you wore an adult’s mask and clothes, unfamiliarized yourself. At journey’s end, a child stood on stairs ahead, hand outstretched. Your clothes no longer fit. You tried to speak; your voice innocent, childlike. The mask shattered; you saw the child’s face closely.

The most pristine smile, seen before. You approached. Garments shattered along the way. Reaching him, he removed his clothes and dressed you. He looked back, walked ahead, vanishing into void.

He vanished, perhaps forever. Yet in the penultimate second before time stilled, his reminder cut through dimness:

“William, help them out. For we are born altruistic beings.”

You left all food supplies at the doorstep, including your night’s ration.

That night, you rushed to Tony’s laboratory. He debugged the time machine. Since 2044, you had secretly traveled back multiple times, trying to prevent Kevin’s death. But each time, his life faded before dawn on August 18, 2024, in a fierce cardiac attack. The only resolution: offer an alternative heart, inject fresh vitality.

“So, that ‘me’ indeed…”

Due to haste, you arrived half a year after the incident in the final travel. To avoid butterfly effects, you posed as Kevin, told friends you’d resurrected and were on leave, severed past ties. On the first August 17 after travel, a hired killer took the heart, preserved it in Tony’s container, now in the basement clay pot.

“He knew I would come! Naples, investigating the dead ‘myself’, his plan? Why choose the formal funeral? He could have told me; I’d have helped.”

Because he was there. At the formal funeral, Kevin rested in the same coffin as you. Your regret was missing his first funeral. This time, you participated personally.

William’s tears fell. Fragments patched together by narrative, he caught up with his doppelgänger. It started because of Kevin, ended because of him. Their fates clung like a bond. “He” was the person on a mission. To uphold Kevin’s priori altruism, “he” offered his life.

“So what’s left for me?” William sobbed, tears soaking pages.

You must understand: he is yourself.

Silence elongated. Only when moonlight galvanized the world, waves quieted day’s clamor, and distant crows cried, did the final line appear:

Use the time machine. Go back before the funeral. Order a heart transplant, knowing it leads to your death. The Observant Camera Obscura shows it. Fate—probability, irreversible. No scientist dares answer why. It’s the stone God cannot lift.

The second method, based on a shared hypothesis, is cruelest:

Terminate his life in the cradle, before meeting us, so we become different people.

William saw the preserved heart in the basement clay pot. It is indeed the time machine itself.

Get in it, close your eyes, visualize the time and location. When you open them, you’ll be there.

He would close his eyes, gently, slowly, recalling the past, and then he smelt sandalwood…
