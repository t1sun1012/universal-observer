As a child, I lingered in doorways, eyes fixated on the spectral men hunched over green tables. Their coins slithered like liquid mercury, vanishing into pockets or strangers’ palms—sometimes forever. My mother’s warnings hissed through our bamboo-walled home: the uncle swallowed by a monsoon night, the neighbor who traded his wife’s gold bangles for ivory dice. Never lean too close to fire or fate, she’d say, her voice brittle as dried reeds. But fire is warm, and fate wears a crooked smile. At sixteen, when the world felt like a cipher waiting to be cracked, I stole a coin from my father’s dresser. In the dim pulse of my bedroom, I flipped it—ninety-eight times—chasing the myth of equilibrium. Gamblers’ arithmetic, I told myself. Not two plus two equals four, but two plus two equals the crackle of a universe that winks. Twice, the coin quivered vertically, suspended between absolutes. I memorized the taut silence of those moments, the way the universe held its breath. Such moments altered my world. I became a little gambler; a real gamble I shall become. Years later, in a tavern slick with spilled ambition, I nursed a drink beneath a flickering bulb. The bartender—a middle-aged Englishman with a moth-eaten collar and fingers stained by nicotine and wine—leaned across the counter. His voice, a gravel-road rasp, unraveled the legend of The Bid God: a stranger with walnut knuckles who’d spun lies of Bolivian silver and Colombian emeralds. “Wrote his family for years,” the Englishman muttered, polishing a glass with a rag dirtier than the floor. “Then came the letter—Send money.”

The stranger had pawned his father’s pocket watch, fled to Vegas. Slots devoured his coins; poker sharks circled his dwindling stack. “Borrowed from me,” the Englishman added, tapping his chest with the mere four fingers on his left hand. “He’d lost it all.” A gaunt figure who’d once mixed wines and fortunes, he’d sworn off gambling after a night that cost him more than money. “Whiskey,” the stranger in the tale demanded, eyes fixed on a grainy racetrack feed. All in on 27—a lame horse, but his lucky number. It was his birthday, October the 27th of a harsh winter devoid of hope. Among the relentless crowd, he was the only prayer. The bystanders’ roar drowned out the casino when the impossibility crossed the finishing line at the speed of light. He repaid his debt, then dissolved into neon static. “Idolized, made manually a legend. If you’d like, call him The Bid God—the players gave him the title,” the Englishman sneered. “But he’s just another ghost I saw, that night and now.” That night, the tavern seethed. Cards hissed; dice rattled like bones. I vowed to stay sober, but the Englishman slid me a whiskey—on the house—and the room blurred. A hand gripped my elbow; laughter, sharp as shrapnel, pulled me into the fray. More and more gamblers got involved. An old hobo stumbled in. People grouped around him, cheering as if they loved him. I sat apart, yet still managed to tax his dry wallet in a poker game. Dawn found me hollowed, the coin in my pocket lighter. Outside, I fumbled for the token. The stranger’s tale coiled in my veins. This is how it begins, I thought. Not with a roar, but a tremor, the split-second before the fall. The coin spun, wobbled, and kissed the pavement. My thoughts fled back to the corner table. The walnut-knuckled tramp slumped there, his spiderlike fingers tracing the rim of an empty glass. “Whiskey,” the stranger demanded again. The Englishman grimaced at his old friend. Recognition prickled—had we crossed paths before?

Or was it the lie we shared, the addiction to the almost, the not quite? None of it mattered. All I knew was this: I’d always known him.
