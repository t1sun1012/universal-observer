The E.P.A. Administrator Andrew Wheeler proposed the agency plans to reduce the number of studies that involve mammal testing by 30 percent by 2025 and to eliminate the studies entirely by 2035. This public denouncement of animal testing gave rise to respective concerns over the issue of animal ethics. Is animal testing morally permissible? Should, and could it be justified? Those are the key argument that I will argue about in this essay.

To better address the problem, animal testing as the central topic must be defined. It is the authorized pharmacy examination to test out medical effects on living beings besides humans. To the extent of political and legislative appropriateness, animal testing is feasible due to the franchised acknowledgment of governments. Up to the current point of history, only 42 countries out of the 193 have passed laws to limit or ban cosmetic animal testing. In areas such as China and Japan, animal testing is still regarded as the most prevalent means to test for the effect of newly invented medicines.

The important point to talk about in the discussion of whether animal testing is justified falls into the category of whether animal testing is ethically applicable. Throughout history, many philosophers had argued against the homogeneity of animals and humans, demanding to draw a clear distinction between humans and animals. For Aristotle, his dualist perspective——a belief that living matters are constituted of two components, minds, and body, material and immaterial——shifts him into arguing about the absence of a “rational soul” in animals (Lehewych, 2019). Within humans and animals is a hierarchy. Descartes furthers this claim with the belief that non-human animals were “insentient” machines. He insisted that animals lack the two components of an immaterial soul, namely thoughts and minds. Humans have subjective experiences because of those immaterial souls inhering from their physical bodies. Animals do not speak or philosophize, and so there are no signs to suggest that they are being inhabited by rational souls, thus, animals, in essence, are complex physical machines without experience, thus they should be regarded as no different from cuckoo clocks or watches. (Kaldas, 2015) In this context, animals being no different from lifeless objects could be used to carry out experiments.

On the contrary, there are philosophers who would back the idea of ‘animal rights.’ Peter Singer, professor of bioethics at Princeton and the University of Melbourne, calls for wide respect for animal rights in the controversial book Animal Liberation. He approached the problem with an unusual means, abandoning the conventional struggle if animals can reason or talk, ‘but can they suffer?’ Said singer. He introduced a religious perspective to view this question, bringing up the Bible to cite his standpoint. The Bible stipulated that God grants humans dominion over animals. The dominion, which is commonly understood as the right to ‘do as we(humans) will’, should be interpreted otherwise as responsible stewardship. (Schacter, 2013) Irrespective of animal rationality, the case of animal consciousness needs also to be brought into the discussion of ethics in animal testing. The evidence to justify an integral animal interest is supported by the anatomical, physiological, and behavioral similarities between humans and animals. “We are animals,” Singer said, citing Darwin’s theory of evolution. With this proclamation, Singer asserts that animals’ interests should be given equal weight to those of humans. Whilst humans have a concern over the potential side effect of certain chemicals, animals, which share the same rights as humans, are being treated as test subjects. According to Singer’s theory, animal testing is neither permissible nor morally justified, as if the tests can be applied to animals, the measures should as well be adopted by human test subjects.

To the extent of my thoughts on animal testing, I believe there is every reason for it to be banned, and I do hold a similar view on this issue with Singer. But Peter Singer avoids the point that Aristotle and Descartes were making on how animals should not be treated as humans because of an immaterial difference, and he goes on to argue that animals deserve equal rights as humans regardless of the difference. The lack of direct confrontation of thoughts is the limitation of Singer’s argument. More logical disapproval of Aristotle and Descartes’s theory is to directly argue against their theory of an immaterial soul is the source of subjective experience. The advancement in biology could influence modern readers of Descartes to critique his pseudo-proposition of some non-physical entity, instead, they are more likely to hold the belief that experiences arise from the brain. The contemporary physiological study of the human and animal brains indicates a high level of structural similarity, proving the likelihood of animals’ exposure to subjective experiences, just like humans, whereas this characteristic has previously been considered ‘exclusive’ to humans. If this argument is embraced, then we cannot conduct inhumane chemical tests that could result in lethal consequences on animals with total insouciance as we might scrap an old lamp or a set of headphones that lack subjectivity for sure.

Some of the stronger criticism of Descartes’ perspective on animals include that from one of his younger contemporary counterparts. Ralph Cudworth (1617-1688), an Anglican theologian, and a keen Classicist argued that thought that Descartes’ view of animals as mindless machines was implausible. He argued instead that animals also have a soul, therefore raising a forthright challenge to Descartes. As Cudworth put it, anyone who looked at animal behavior which he claimed to be of incredible variety and complexity and decided that it is all merely physical mechanism “will never be able clearly to defend the incorporeity and immortality of human souls” (Cudworth,1678). To put it bluntly, if animals carry out their acts purely due to their physical makeup, human behaviors are probably driven the same way. If Descartes deemed animal behaviors as resulting from nothing but ‘blood and brains’, humans would have fallen under the same category as animals under his definition.

Cudworth’s argument pinpoints the weakness of Descartes’s standpoint on the issue. According to him, Descartes’ mistake was that his conception of the soul was too narrow.

Animals do not have to prove their capacity for a soul by speaking or thinking reflectively; rather, their sophisticated surviving mechanisms could have been the result of nothing else but a soul. A dualist counterattack seems to persuade in the context, yet, it has a bias, one that assumes the presence of souls as the source of conscious minds due to the orthodox assumption in his era. Also, animal testing had yet to become prevalent. Given the context, Cudworth’s argument also has its limitation. Instead of using the statement that neither humans nor animals have a soul, and they are biologically alike, he tried to show that animals also have a soul; but Cudworth did commit to bridging Descartes’ radical gap between humans and animals. He believed that although animals do have a soul, it is less perfect and less conscious than a human soul. Given its existence, we are given more moral responsibilities towards animals than we are towards soulless, mindless objects. There is a fundamental difference between humans and animals, as the souls of the latter are in less complex, less developed forms. Cudworth admitted the distinction but put forward the idea that the special completeness of human souls does not entail the worthlessness of animal ones. Following his definition of animal rights, animal testing which does harm to animals is an act of demolishing our responsibilities owing to them. Thus, it is not morally permissible.

Philosophers hold contradicting positions on the issue of animal rights, and some of the deeper implications of the question extend to modern society, in the form of the discussion of whether animal testing should be banned. Ideas from an earlier age commonly point to the saying that animals are inferior to humans because they lack a soul, but amongst them, opposing voices exist. Cudworth rejected the claim made by Descartes and Aristotle. He criticized their view from a dualist perspective, arguing that animals have a soul. Being less than a human doesn’t grant the latter a privilege to exert a negative influence on the vulnerable group. Modern philosophers like Singer cut into the problem with a scientific prerequisite. They argue that with the structural similarities between humans and animals, the two should share equal interests. Animal rights ought not to be infringed with malignant chemical tests. Personally, I believe that animal testing is never justified. As a student of biology and philosophy, I place animal ethics at the same height as human ethics. I recognized how humans evolve from animals but maintain an underlying connection with them. We possess many common features, including the capacity for emotion, the ability to form a social web, and the 7 basic criteria of living beings. Given all that, we should not carry out any chemical tests on animals. They are suffering, being more or less close to them, I can tell.
