‘Are you suffering?’

Yes

‘And are you satisfied with that?’

Yes

‘What about your little sisters? They’ve also gone through the 21 families, always get rejected in the end.’

They deserve a better life.

So, why not make a decision now…

We are the excluded. We are not needed in this indifferent society. Does the tragedy start from the moment we are born, or when our parents passed away for the reason only God knows? That doesn’t matter. We are orphans. We grow in foster care, yet never get truly accepted. In the different rooms covered with a boundless shadow, strangers whisper beside the corner, leaving only my siblings and me in the middle of those empty spaces. Following the alternation of seasons, years by years, that happens to be it.

He was no more than a tiny guy, 12 years old, but there is his other role——he is the eldest in his family. He carries the responsibility of uniting the whole family out of his strong awareness of duty. He knows he is a steersman, holding the wheel of his sibling’s fate on that skinny arm.

‘You have one chance to bestow felicity upon the girls. They will find their spot in a new family. They will get their room and beds. They will forever stay away from the vagrant lifestyle. Now the decision is on you. You are in charge of your family.’

I’ve had a few conversations with the man in front of me in the same stuffy office. He’s the representative of those people. He never tells me his name and demands me to call him ‘A’. He informs me the first time we meet that somebody is willing to offer a room to my two little sisters so that they could have a legitimate place to live, instead of having to roam across every hidden corner of the streets. To be honest, there can’t be a better deal.

But I’d hesitate, once, twice, and this is the third time.

Back to now.

I point to the ground with my fingers shaking slightly, then gingerly raise them, folding my hands firmly. My mouth trembles out the same phrases all over again, ’Please take the rest of us as well. We don’t need a room or beds. All we want is to keep the family integral.’

‘No’, says the man again with a rather contemptuous look, ’they could only afford to take the two youngest sisters.’

It was at that moment when he realizes there was no further space for negotiation. Three times in a row, absolutely no results.

He gritted his teeth and sighed. The veins on his forehead burst. From time to time, he shook his head and pounded the cold ebony desk. In the end, there’s nothing he could do.

‘For the sake of your lives, C and D. Promise me to stay away from the old grief. Your new life is starting.’ He said to himself with a solemn tone, almost as if his sisters would hear. Then he reached out for the piece of contract lying silently in front of him. He picked up the pen of a trenchant nib, poking the heavy load of papers as thick as his heart.

‘Goodbye. I hope this is not the last time.’

The door is left open, but the man in front of me is gone, along with my two little sisters. I feel eddies of sorrow uprising from the back of my heart. The decision has been made. It’s time to live on.

He stood up and went out of the door. The dusk was falling. The persisting clamor of the streets faded, leaving only the crows cawing from the far end of the hill. The day is about to end.
